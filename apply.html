The Presidential Innovation Fellows (PIF) program pairs top innovators from the private sector, non-profits, and academia with change agents in the federal government to solve our nation's most pressing problems. PIFs are deployed across government on short-term "tours of duty" to develop solutions that save lives, save money, and fuel job creation.

Building a 21st Century Veterans Experience

The Veterans Administration (VA) is embarking on a bold new effort not just to eliminate the benefits backlog, but to create a “digital by default” experience that meets or exceeds veterans’ expectations of service in the 21st offices within the VA will need to better understand what is possible with modern approaches to service delivery enabled by lightweight, consumer-focused technology platforms and Application Programming Interfaces (APIs). Presidential Innovation Fellows will work with and across key programs to find agile, strategic, and efficient ways to integrate disparate services into a single user experience. Fellows will serve as scouts and ambassadors to aid several departments within the VA in creating pilot programs, changing expectations about what is possible, finding early wins, and creating a roadmap towards an integrated experience that meets veterans’ needs.

By the People, For the People: Crowdsourcing to Improve Government

In an age when the needs of society outweigh the resources available to government, crowdsourcing offers the promise of a pool of resources that scale with the size of the community. This slate of five projects at five different agencies is designed to test different models of crowdsourcing in different contexts and share lessons learned across the cohort, with the goal of suggesting future opportunities to explore this strategy across the federal government in the years to come. The five projects are:

  1. Open Street Map for Diplomacy - Department of State

    OpenStreetMap (OSM) is a collaborative project to create a free editable map of the world. In disasters like the Haiti earthquake, OSM allows volunteers around the world to quickly build maps of uncharted places that make it possible to get help to where it is most needed. OSM for Diplomacy expands on existing State Department initiatives to create and share open geographic data with the OSM community and support the growth of global technology communities at events hosted around the globe. This project aims to make citizen mapping a core component of engagement with civil society and openly share the outcomes to benefit humanitarian and development work globally. In a larger sense, we hope to show how open collaboration can transform the relationship between civil society and government.

  2. Asteroid Grand Challenge - National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA)

    Earlier this summer, NASA announced the Asteroid Grand Challenge to: Find All Asteroid Threats to Human Populations and Know What to Do About Them. Recognizing the power of traditional and innovative collaboration – including the use of public-private partnerships, citizen science, crowdsourcing, and incentive prizes, NASA seeks to build the processes and infrastructure to communicate, coordinate, and implement this massive endeavor. This project will be part of a larger effort around enabling new measurements and data from citizen scientists around the world to contribute to the Grand Challenge and to make existing data from space and ground-based observatories open and machine-readable. The project could also encompass a tracking system for volunteers to log their contributions and potentially earn “badges” to recognize their work.

  3. Crowdsourcing Tools to Unlock Government Records - National Archives and Record Administration (NARA)

    With more than 12 billion pages of records, the National Archives and Records Administration has as tremendous challenge helping citizens find exactly what they are looking for in government records. Crowdsourcing tools help unlock the information contained within the records so that the information can actually be used. The agency’s current crowdsourcing efforts include the Citizen Archivist Dashboard, transcription projects, scan-a-thons, and partnerships with Wikipedia. This project seeks to engage citizen developers and civic hacking communities in the open development of these and other tools to benefit not only the National Archives, but crowdsourcing efforts across the government.

  4. Using the Crowd to Improve Patent Quality - United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO)

    To ensure the integrity of the US patents process, United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) patent examiners must find and apply information about the state-of-the-art in determining whether an invention is patent-worthy. A provision of the recently-enacted America Invents Act allows for “third party submissions,” or contributions by the public of information relevant to the patent submission. Though crowdsourcing websites have been established to encourage public involvement in the patenting process, use of these sites to make meaningful contributions has been limited. This project will focus on identifying and piloting effective approaches to tap into the relevant innovation communities through a wide variety of mechanisms. The goal is an increase in the volume and relevance of information submitted, both of which will in turn increase patent quality, which is vital to innovation and economic growth.

  5. Crowdsourcing Disaster Response Information - Department of Energy

    During a disaster, tapping the power of information reported through social media can significantly improve situational awareness and speed disaster response and recovery efforts. That’s why the White House, the Federal Emergency Management Agency, and the Department of Energy launched standardized hash tags (#PowerLineDown #NoFuel and #GotFuel) to enable citizens to report downed power lines and the fuel status of gas stations via social media platforms. This project seeks to make this citizen-reported information available and actionable to FEMA, the Department of Energy, survivors, first responders, state and local officials, and utilities through a variety of platforms. One such platform is Lantern, a new mobile app from DOE that will allow survivors to report and access information on power outages and fallen power lines and help users find fuel and report the status of gas stations.

Data Innovation

Previously known as the Open Data and MyData initiatives, the Data Innovation effort focuses on accelerating and expanding efforts to make the government’s information resources more publicly accessible in machine-readable formats and spurring the use of those resources by entrepreneurs as fuel for the creation of new products, services, and jobs. Also within this effort are projects that focus on empowering the American people with secure access to their own personal health, energy, and education, and financial data (MyData initiatives). This suite of Data Innovation initiatives will support the President’s Climate Data Initiative, a new Skills and Training Data Initiative, and other priority data initiatives across government. The Data Innovation cohort will recruit Presidential Innovation Fellows to serve in the following agencies:

  • National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA)
  • Census Bureau
  • National Aeronautic and Space Administration (NASA)
  • Department of the Interior (DOI)
  • Department of Labor (DOL)
  • Internal Revenue Service (IRS)
  • National Institutes of Health (NIH)
  • Department of Energy (DOE)
  • Department of Health and Human Services (HHS)
